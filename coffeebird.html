The Problem
what's the problem?
Coffee drinkers struggle to balance convenience, confidence, and personal needs (energy, flavor, or sensitivity), leading to inconsistent, stressful, or unsatisfying coffee experiences.
The Problem
Study Goal Interview
Insight
Coffee drinkers want coffee that fits their energy level, mood, and routine, but their confidence, time, and knowledge vary widely, making it difficult to choose or prepare the "right" cup consistently.
  • "When I feel too down, I want…more sweetness coffee, like caramel macchiato." —SungJu. Coffee choice motivated by mood.
  • "The most important thing…is its potency, which is why I mainly stick to black coffee…The two biggest things are cost and effect." —David, coffee choice is motivated by energy needs and affordability.
Need
A simple, reliable, and personalized coffee experience that matches their desired taste, caffeine level, and effort, without requiring expert skills.
  • "I prefer fresh coffee…if a coffee is roasted more than a month ago, I would just give it a pass." — Andrew, prioritizes freshly roasted and freshly ground beans.
  • "I'm not a barista… I just want it to be easy." — Sungju, prefers fast, simple preparation and dislikes complicated brewing steps.
Pain
Users struggle with complicated equipment, uncertainty about caffeine strength or brewing steps, and negative effects (jitters, anxiety, bad sleep), leading to frustration or avoidance.
  • "I have no confidence adjusting grind size." — David, prefer pre-ground or capsule-based solutions due to lack of confidence in grinding process.
  • "Too much caffeine makes me anxious or dizzy." "My machine broke and it was a terrible experience." — Sungju, pain with coffee due to side effects and frustration with machine issues.
Desire
To enjoy coffee in a way that feels easy, safe, and satisfying, whether that means energizing focus, comforting warmth, or playful flavor exploration.
  • "I love trying new flavors when the shop recommends them." — Andrew, coffee journey began with the surprise of discovering flavor diversity.
  • "I want coffee that actually helps me start my day." — Sungju, drinks coffee for energy, productivity, and motivation.
  • "In China many coffee shops add juice or soft drink in it." — Zi, values shops that offer experimental customization.
The Goal
Long Term Goal
People can enjoy coffee without frustration, anxiety, or exclusion.
Theme exploration of how to achieve the goal

Convenience & Confidence

Across interviews, users consistently want coffee to be easy, fast, and low-effort, but many also lack confidence in brewing or choosing the "right" coffee.

Caffeine Awareness & Safety

Multiple users expressed struggles with caffeine sensitivity, anxious reactions, or not knowing how strong their coffee is.

Personalization & Joy

Users want coffee to match their mood, energy, taste preference, and emotional state — whether they want novelty, comfort, or exploration.

Ideats
Expert Research Interview Snapshot
Insight
Coffee drinkers want a cup that adapts to their time, mood, and confidence level, but their knowledge, tools, and routines vary widely. Users rely heavily on taste as an emotional anchor, and their trust in the process is shaped by predictability, convenience, and guidance.
  • "If the coffee doesn't taste good, it will actually ruin my day." —Andrew, taste is emotionally consequential.
  • "The lowest experience is instant coffee, no flavor, but no flaws." —Konglang, when reliability drops, users prefer predictability over quality.
  • "I need guidance when I'm unfamiliar with a type of coffee." —Shuang, exploration is appealing but requires clear, confidence-building support.
Need
Users need consistent, good-tasting coffee delivered with minimal effort, clear guidance, and enough flexibility to match their lifestyle and energy demands. Whether beginners or experts, they want a system that helps them succeed without stress.
  • "First it has to be rich but not too strong…and healthy." — Shuang, baseline taste and health requirements must be met every time.
  • "I'd want the machine to remember my taste—grind size, temperature." — Andrew, prefers fast, simple preparation.
  • "It shouldn't take too long." — Konglang, speed and simplicity are essential in daily routines.
Pain
Users struggle with inconsistent results, equipment issues, long wait times, and a lack of clarity when choosing or preparing coffee. These challenges create anxiety, frustration, or wasted effort.
  • "Stale beans are the most frustrating part." — Andrew, quality degrades quickly, creating unpredictable results.
  • "They made the wrong kind of coffee for me." — Shuang, ordering errors break trust in external coffee services.
  • "Cheap commercial coffee has too many flaws." — Konglang, low-quality options feel unpleasant and disappointing.
Desire
Users want a coffee experience that feels intuitive, enjoyable, and customizable, one that supports mood, energy levels, and personal rituals. They desire automation that removes friction, along with opportunities for exploration when they have time.
  • "I want a high-end automated machine so I don't have to hustle around the kitchen." —Shuang, automation reduces cognitive load.
  • "The machine should remember my preferences." — Andrew, long-term personalization deepens satisfaction.
  • "I study techniques to make it taste as good as possible." — Konglang, expert users desire tools that preserve control and precision.

User Testing
1st User Testing Feedback
Insight
Users deeply value the app's emotional warmth, personality, and artistic identity — it sets the experience apart and builds instant delight. But the app must strengthen structural clarity, ensuring first-time users immediately understand where to start and how to proceed.
  • "The flow is seamless when I know where to click." —SungJu
  • "It's vivid and cheerful… but I didn't know what to do at first." —Andrew
  • "It would be more intuitive if recommendations and shopping worked together." —Shuang
Liked
Users were drawn to the app's warm, coffee-inspired aesthetic and friendly personality, which made the experience feel inviting, modern, and delightfully crafted. They appreciated how intuitive the flow felt when navigating recommendations and customization.
  • "I really love the brownish color and your character. I can understand everything without your lead." —SungJu
  • "This is exactly what I wanted. The customization is very nice." —Andrew
  • "It looks calm and reminds me of the smell of coffee." —Shuang
Critique
The main friction came from unclear hierarchy and visual overload, especially on the landing page, where users struggled to know where to look or begin. Some interaction points — like the GPT input field — were easy to miss.
  • "As a first-time user, I don't know where to land my eyes… too many things on the screen." —Andrew
  • "It would be more intuitive if recommendations and shopping worked together." —Shuang
Suggestions
Users consistently recommended simplifying the landing page to establish a clear primary action, supported by stronger visual hierarchy and reduced clutter. Highlighting the GPT input field and unifying icon styles would help users feel more guided.
  • "Maybe you can put different colors so it's more intuitive for users." —SungJu
  • "I need to read everything top to bottom before knowing what to do." —Andrew
  • "These icons at the bottom don't match—maybe replace them." —Shuang

User Testing
2nd User Testing Feedback
Insight
Both testers emphasized the need for clarity, confidence, and emotional reassurance. Andrew noted that "If the taste is off, it can actually ruin my whole day," showing how sensitive users are to uncertainty. Shuang added that "I just need guidance when I'm unfamiliar," reinforcing that users want support but not overwhelm. A clear, predictable flow reduces anxiety and strengthens trust.
Liked
Testers appreciated the clean layout and intuitive structure. Andrew said "this looks clean, makes me want to continue," while Shuang shared "I can see everything at a glance and don't have to guess." They also liked how visuals and step-by-step flow made the experience feel approachable and easy to follow.
Critique
Moments of confusion arose around unclear labels and heavier text sections. Andrew mentioned being unsure "what this button does," and Shuang noted she "thought this would show something else." Both felt some text was too long — Andrew said "this is a bit much to read," while Shuang admitted "too long, I skim."
Suggestions
Users wanted clearer labeling, shorter text, and optional guidance. Shuang suggested "just a quick description would help," pointing to the need for lightweight hints. Andrew's confusion with unclear buttons supports adding more explicit actions. Overall, recommendations lean toward micro-guidance, clearer hierarchy, and more digestible content.

Key Learnings
What do I learn?
I learned that less is more — overwhelming users will just distract them from the important features.
Details matter — having a cohesive set of icons, color coding, etc., all contributes to how the user feels when using the product.
Designing for a domain you can't personally experience requires deeper reliance on research — and the outsider perspective often surfaces insights that insiders overlook.
Opportunity
Future Opportunities
Coffee Bird has a lot of potential. From what I learned with this case study, I'd love to branch out to different kinds of drinks to provide a wider variety of beverage experiences.
The community aspect was commonly brought up during interviews and testing — adding a community feature where people can share their coffee experiences and recipes would be a great enhancement to users' coffee experiences.
